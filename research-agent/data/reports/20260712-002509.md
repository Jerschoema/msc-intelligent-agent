# Research Brief: federated learning for medical imaging

_Run `20260712-002509` · 4 source(s) summarised._

**Research question:** what are the privacy risks?

## Introduction

The integration of federated learning (FL) into medical imaging presents significant opportunities for collaborative healthcare advancements while simultaneously raising critical questions about privacy risks. This brief addresses the research question of what privacy risks arise in federated learning for medical imaging by synthesizing insights from four recent sources. The analysis focuses on the unique challenges of medical data sensitivity and the specific mechanisms that could compromise patient privacy in distributed FL systems.

## Summary

All sources acknowledge federated learning's potential to mitigate privacy risks through decentralized data handling, but they diverge on the nature and severity of these risks in medical imaging contexts. [1] emphasizes VAFL's perturbed local embedding as a robust privacy mechanism with theoretical guarantees, while [2] identifies model poisoning and data leakage as primary threats to federated transfer learning systems. [3] highlights FL's principle of data decentralization as a privacy safeguard, yet notes the field's rapid growth and unresolved challenges. [4] reveals that deep learning models in federated settings leak significant training data information through parameter updates, enabling active membership inference attacks despite high model accuracy.

## Findings

### 1. VAFL: a Method of Vertical Asynchronous Federated Learning
*Tianyi Chen, Xiao Jin, Yuejiao Sun, Wotao Yin (2020)*

_Source reputation: medium — Open-access preprint server; dominant venue for ML/physics/CS research but not peer-reviewed_

_Based on the abstract only — the full text was not accessible._

This research introduces VAFL, a vertical asynchronous federated learning method designed to handle multi-client data with diverse feature sets. The method enables clients to independently run stochastic gradient algorithms without coordination, making it suitable for intermittent connectivity scenarios. It incorporates perturbed local embedding for data privacy and communication efficiency, with theoretical guarantees for convergence and privacy across different objective types. Empirical results show VAFL outperforms both centralized and synchronous FL methods on image and healthcare datasets.

**Key claims:**
- VAFL enables clients to run stochastic gradient algorithms without coordination
  > each client to run stochastic gradient algorithms without coordination with other clients
- VA:FL uses perturbed local embedding for data privacy and communication efficiency
  > a new technique of perturbed local embedding to ensure data privacy and improve communication efficiency
- VAFL provides theoretical convergence rates and privacy guarantees for strongly convex, nonconvex, and nonsmooth objectives
  > theoretically, we present the convergence rate and privacy level of our method for strongly convex, nonconvex and even nonsmooth objectives separately
- VAFL outperforms centralized and synchronous FL methods on image and healthcare datasets
  > The results compare favorably to centralized and synchronous FL methods

### 2. Federated and Transfer Learning: A Survey on Adversaries and Defense Mechanisms
*Ehsan Hallaji, Roozbeh Razavi-Far, Mehrdad Saif (2022)*

_Source reputation: medium — Open-access preprint server; dominant venue for ML/physics/CS research but not peer-reviewed_

This survey examines the intersection of federated learning (FL) and transfer learning (TL) from a security perspective, highlighting vulnerabilities and defense mechanisms. The authors identify that FL addresses privacy concerns by decentralizing data, but FTL (federated TL) overcomes FL's limitation of requiring similar features across clients. The survey categorizes FL into horizontal, vertical, and federated transfer learning variants and discusses threats like model poisoning and data leakage that could compromise privacy and performance in FTL systems.

**Key claims:**
- Federated learning (FL) allows collaborative model training across client devices while decentralizing training data to address privacy concerns. (p. 2)
  > FL allows for the collaborative training of a DL model across a network of client devices. This multi-party collaboration is accomplished by communication with a central server and decentralisation of training data [71].
- Federated transfer learning (FTL) enables knowledge transfer between clients with differing feature spaces, overcoming FL's constraint of similar attributes. (p. 4)
  > FTL facilitates knowledge transfer between participants when the overlap between sample and feature space is minimal [64].
- The primary threats to FTL include model poisoning attacks that corrupt the training process and data leakage that compromises privacy. (p. 5)
  > During the training process, communicating model updates might divulge critical information and lead to deep leakage. This can consequently jeopardize the privacy of local data or lead to high-jacking the training data [118].
- FTL systems require secure frameworks to protect against privacy breaches without sacrificing model accuracy. (p. 4)
  > A secure FTL framework is proposed in [64] that uses secret sharing and homomorphic encryption to protect privacy without sacrificing accuracy, which is a typical issue in privacy-preserving techniques.

### 4. Comprehensive Privacy Analysis of Deep Learning: Passive and Active White-box Inference Attacks against Centralized and Federated Learning
*Milad Nasr, Reza Shokri, Amir Houmansadr (2019)*

_Source reputation: medium — Open scholarly metadata index (aggregator, not a publisher)_

_Based on the abstract only — the full text was not accessible._

This research presents a comprehensive privacy analysis of deep learning models using white-box inference attacks for both centralized and federated learning settings. The study demonstrates that deep learning models leak significant information about their training data, even well-generalized models, through parameter updates and trained model parameters. The authors design new white-box membership inference attacks that exploit vulnerabilities in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm, showing that straightforward extensions of black-box attacks are ineffective in this context. Their analysis reveals that adversarial participants in federated learning can successfully perform active membership inference attacks against others despite high model prediction accuracy.

**Key claims:**
- Deep learning models leak significant information about their training data
  > Deep neural networks are susceptible to various inference attacks as they remember information about their training data.
- Well-generalized models are significantly susceptible to white-box membership inference attacks
  > We show that even well-generalized models are significantly susceptible to white. 
- Adversarial participants in federated learning can successfully run active membership inference attacks
  > We also show how adversarial participants, in the federated learning setting, can successfully run active membership inference attacks against other participants, even when the global model achieves high prediction accuracies.
- Straightforward extensions of black-box attacks to white-box setting are ineffective
  > We show that a straightforward extension of the known black-box attacks to the white-box setting (through analyzing the outputs of activation functions) is ineffective.

## Conclusion

The evidence indicates that federated learning for medical imaging introduces significant privacy risks, particularly through mechanisms like model poisoning, data leakage, and membership inference attacks, which can compromise patient privacy even when using advanced privacy-preserving techniques such as perturbed local embeddings.

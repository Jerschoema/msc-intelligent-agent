# Research Brief: contrastive learning for image representation

_Run `20260711-185920` · 2 source(s) summarised._

## Synthesis

Both papers explore contrastive learning for image representation but address different aspects: [1] focuses on enhancing robustness through counterfactual augmentations for medical imaging, while [2] provides theoretical insights into embedding alignment and batch-size effects. They agree that standard contrastive learning has limitations in capturing realistic domain variations (as highlighted in [1]), but [2] shifts the focus to intra-embedding dynamics rather than domain shifts. The field appears to be converging on the importance of robust representation learning, yet the two papers operate in distinct contexts—application-driven (medical) versus theoretical (embedding properties).

**Key takeaways:**
- Counterfactual contrastive learning improves robustness against domain shifts in medical imaging by generating realistic counterfactual images [1]
- Perfect positive pair alignment is unattainable in full-batch settings due to negative-pair similarity thresholds [2]
- Smaller batch sizes increase negative-pair variance, degrading representation quality, and an auxiliary loss mitigates this [2]

**Open questions:**
- How do counterfactual augmentations interact with batch-size effects in contrastive learning?
- Can the theoretical insights from [2] be applied to improve robustness in medical imaging (addressed by [1])?

## Findings

### 1. Robust image representations with counterfactual contrastive learning
*Mélanie Roschewitz, Fabio De Sousa Ribeiro, Tian Xia, Galvin Khara, Ben Glocker (2024)*

_Source reputation: medium — Open-access preprint server; dominant venue for ML/physics/CS research but not peer-reviewed_

The paper introduces counterfactual contrastive learning as a novel framework for robust image representation learning, particularly addressing limitations in medical imaging where standard contrastive learning's image augmentations fail to capture realistic domain variations. By leveraging causal image generation models to create counterfactual images that simulate plausible domain shifts (e.g., scanner differences), the approach generates cross-domain positive pairs that encourage representations to ignore acquisition-specific variations. This method improves robustness against distribution shifts compared to traditional contrastive learning that relies on handcrafted augmentations.

**Key claims:**
- Contrastive pretraining can substantially increase model generalisation and downstream performance. (p. 1)
- Traditional contrastive pipelines attempt to simulate domain shifts through pre-defined generic image transformations. (p. 1)
- These do not always mimic realistic and relevant domain variations for medical imaging, such as scanner differences. (p. 1)
- We herein introduce counterfactual contrastive learning, a novel framework leveraging recent advances in causal image synthesis to improve robustness. (p. 1)

### 2. On the Similarities of Embeddings in Contrastive Learning
*Chungpa Lee, Sehee Lim, Kibok Lee, Jy-yong Sohn (2025)*

_Source reputation: medium — Open-access preprint server; dominant venue for ML/physics/CS research but not peer-reviewed_

_Based on the abstract only — the full text was not accessible._

This paper introduces a theoretical framework for understanding contrastive learning using cosine similarity. It identifies two key phenomena: first, that perfect positive pair alignment is unattainable in full-batch settings when negative-pair similarities fall below a threshold, which can be mitigated by including within-view negatives; second, that smaller batch sizes increase negative-p  pair variance, degrading representation quality, and proposes an auxiliary loss to reduce this variance. The study demonstrates that this loss improves performance in small-batch settings.

**Key claims:**
- Perfect alignment of positive pairs is unattainable in full-batch settings when negative-pair similarities fall below a threshold
- Incorporating within-view negative pairs mitigates misalignment in full-batch settings
- Smaller batch sizes induce stronger separation among negative pairs in embedding space
- Higher variance in negative-pair similarities degrades representation quality in mini-batch settings

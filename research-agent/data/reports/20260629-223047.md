### 1. Clustering Multivariate Time Series using Energy Distance
*Richard A. Davis, Leon Fernandes, Konstantinos Fokianos (2023)*

This paper introduces a novel nonparametric methodology for clustering multivariate time series data using energy distance, as defined by Székely and Rizzo (2013). The method calculates a dissimilarity matrix based on (h+1)-dimensional joint distributions of the time series components. This matrix is then utilized with hierarchical clustering to group similar time series, demonstrating superior performance in nonlinear and multivariate VAR scenarios compared to traditional methods.

**Key claims:**
- The energy distance measure effectively handles heavy-tailed data, unlike many other dissimilarity measures. (p. 4)
  > Although we assume the components are of equal length, this is only for the sake of convenience; the results of this paper can be easily extended to the situation where the lengths of the time series vary across components.
- The methodology forms a pairwise dissimilarity matrix based on (h+1)-dimensional joint distributions, followed by hierarchical clustering. (p. 8)
  > Consider observations {X1, X2,..., Xn} from a multivariate time series Xt = (Xt1,Xt2,...,X td)T in Rd. In this section, a general methodology for clustering component time series based on (h + 1)-dimensional distributions, for a fixed lag h≥ 0, is presented.
- The energy distance dissimilarity measure utilizes the joint distribution of lagged components to quantify separation between time series. (p. 8)
  > Djk = ˆdE(Y,Z ), where Yt = (Xt,j,Xt+1,j,...,X t+h,j)T and Zt = (Xt,k,Xt+1,k,...,X t+h,k)T for t = 1,…,n−h. The energy distance dissimilarity measure is given by Djk := ˆdE(Y,Z ); see also Fokianos and Pitsillou (2018).
- The proposed procedure exhibits superior clustering performance in nonlinear and multivariate VAR time series compared to other methods. (p. 5)
  > Our proposed procedure performed generally better when clustering nonlinear and multivariate VAR time series than other methods.

### 2. JANET: Joint Adaptive predictioN-region Estimation for Time-series
*Eshant English, Eliot Wong-Toi, Matteo Fontana, Stephan Mandt, Padhraic Smyth, Christoph Lippert (2024)*

JANET (Joint Adaptive predictioN-region Estimation for Time-series) represents a novel framework designed to construct valid prediction regions for time series data, addressing limitations of existing methods. This system allows for both univariate and multivariate time series analysis, adapting its prediction regions based on historical context and crucially controlling the K-familywise error rate with finite-sample guarantees.  JANET leverages inductive conformalisation and is particularly useful in multi-step ahead prediction tasks, offering improved uncertainty quantification compared to traditional approaches. The framework’s efficiency and adaptability have been demonstrated through empirical evaluations across diverse datasets.

**Key claims:**
- JANET can handle both single and multiple time series (univariate or multivariate) whilst providing adaptive prediction regions based on historical context (lagged values), and controlling K-familywise error with finite-sample guarantees. (p. 17)
  > To the best of our knowledge, JANET is the only framework that can handle both single and multiple time series (univariate or multivariate), whilst providing adaptive prediction regions based on historical context (lagged values), and controlling K-familywise error with finite-sample guarantees.
- JANET is capable of producing prediction regions with marginal intervals that adapt to both the time horizon and the relevant historical context of the data. (p. 17)
  > JANET is capable of producing prediction regions with marginal intervals that adapt to both the time horizon and the relevant historical context of the data.
- The framework, JANET, guarantees validity as the permutation across independent time series is exchangeable, whilst for single time series it provides approximate validity under weak assumptions on the non-conformity scores. (p. 3)
  > When applied to multiple independent time series, JANET guarantees validity as the permutation across independent time series is exchangeable, whilst, for single time series, it provides approximate validity under weak assumptions on the non-conformity scores as long as transformations (permutations within a single series) of the data are a meaningful approximation for a stationary series.

### 3. LLM-Mixer: Multiscale Mixing in LLMs for Time Series Forecasting
*Md Kowsher, Md. Shohanur Islam Sobuj, Nusrat Jahan Prottasha, E. Alejandro Alanis, Ozlem Ozmen Garibay, Niloofar Yousefi (2024)*

This research introduces LLM-Mixer, a new framework that combines multiscale time series decomposition with pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) to improve forecasting accuracy. The system leverages multiple temporal resolutions of the data through downsampling and processes them with a frozen LLM guided by a text prompt. Experimental results demonstrate that LLM-Mixer outperforms several baseline models across various datasets, including ETTh1, ETTh2, Electricity, Traffic, and PEMS datasets, particularly excelling in both short and long-term forecasting.

**Key claims:**
- LLM-Mixer achieves favorable performance compared to other models like TIME-LLM, TimeMixer, and PatchTST across various time series datasets. (p. 5)
  > Compared to other models such as TIME-LLM, TimeMixer, and PatchTST, LLM-Mixer performs favorably...
- Average pooling consistently yields lower MSE values when decomposing time series data into multiscale representations. (p. 7)
  > Notably, average pooling consistently yielded a lower MSE, suggesting that this method is better suited for capturing multi-scale dependencies in the data.
- LLM-Mixer demonstrates competitive accuracy across short-term multivariate forecasting tasks using datasets such as PEMS03, PEMS04 and PEMS07. (p. 5)
  > Specifically, LLM-Mixer demonstrates competitive accuracy on PEMS03, PEMS04, and PEMS07, outperforming several baseline models...
- The LLM-Mixer consistently achieves the lowest MSE and MAE values when forecasting univariate time series data across datasets like ETT. (p. 7)
  > LLM-Mixer achieves the lowest MSE and MAE values across all datasets, consistently outperforming other methods like Linear, NLinear, and FEDformer.

### 4. Forecasting intermittent time series with Gaussian Processes and Tweedie likelihood
*Stefano Damato, Dario Azzimonti, Giorgio Corani (2025)*

This research explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for probabilistic forecasting of intermittent time series, moving beyond traditional point forecasts. The study utilizes a Bayesian framework and evaluates two likelihood functions: the negative binomial and the Tweedie distributions. Results demonstrate that models incorporating the Tweedie distribution, particularly TweedieGP, consistently provide more reliable probabilistic forecasts due to their ability to accurately estimate high quantiles.

**Key claims:**
- The Tweedie distribution offers greater flexibility than the negative binomial in capturing the characteristics of intermittent time series. (p. 18)
  > While the negative binomial has already been used in forecasting intermittent time series, this is the first time in which a fully parameterized Tweedie density is used for intermittent time series. TweedieGP obtains the best estimates of the highest quantiles, thus showing that it is more flexible than NegBinGP.
- Probabilistic forecasting based on probability distributions, rather than point forecasts, is preferable for modeling intermittent time series. (p. 18)
  > The prediction of intermittent time series should be based on probability distributions rather than on point forecasts.
- TweedieGP consistently outperforms other models in estimating high quantiles, which is crucial for accurate forecasting of intermittent count time series. (p. 3)
  > In particular, TweedieGP often outperforms the competitors on the highest quantiles, the most difficult to estimate.
- The Tweedie likelihood allows for a proper representation of the heavy tails inherent in intermittent count time series data, avoiding simplification assumptions made by existing models (p. 1)
  > We properly evaluate the Tweedie density, which has both a point mass at zero and heavy tails, avoiding simplifying assumptions made in existing models.
